Service description

Find a solution to an employment dispute

Use this service to start the legal process in which you tell Acas that you want to:

  • try our free early conciliation service
  • get a certificate to make a claim to an employment tribunal

This service allows claimants or their representatives to notify Acas of a workplace dispute and choose whether they want early conciliation or to get a certificate to the employment tribunal for a judge to decide. It is a statutory service, and claimants cannot access the employment tribunal without first making a notification to Acas. Acas receives around 120,000 notifications a year.

Early conciliation is a largely telephone-based service that involves speaking with individuals and organisations in the dispute. Highly-trained conciliators help parties understand relevant legal principles and assist them to reach a resolution.

If a resolution cannot be found, or the claimant does not want conciliation, then Acas provides a certificate that allows parties to take their dispute to the employment tribunal.

Service users

Claimants and Representatives

Overview of service users

  • “Claimants” – individuals with a workplace dispute
  • “Representatives” – solicitors, legal advisors, trades’ union representatives or family members/friends who speak to Acas on behalf of a claimant

High level user needs this service aims to meet

  1. As a claimant I need to tell Acas about my workplace dispute so that I can take my employer to an employment tribunal or reach a settlement through early conciliation
  2. As a representative I need to tell Acas about a workplace dispute so that a claimant can take their employer to an employment tribunal or reach a settlement through early conciliation

2. Solve a whole problem for users

How the service intends to solve one whole problem for users, collaborating across organisational boundaries where necessary.

Problem to be solved 1:

When we spoke to early conciliation support officers and conciliators, they told us that every day, they get duplicate submissions from the same users and most of the time it is incomplete. That is, the same users were submitting the form multiple times. In fact, one of the early conciliation support offers said that on the day when we spoke to her, one user had submitted the same form 4 times. When asked how often this occurs? She said “90% of the time. ”. When we were observing the early conciliation support officer take calls from the users, we saw first-hand how frustrated the users were when they would call in to say that they had submitted form two times, but the early conciliation support officer still did not have all the information. Then the officer had to collect the information all over again, over the phone. After the early conciliation support officer took the data from the user, she had to search for multiple incomplete entries, which the user had made, then close these cases and pick the reason as “duplicate” for closing the cases. They said the reason this was happening is that users were not sure if they had submitted the form when they were moving to form two as this confused the user.

How we solved the problem 1:

As you can see in the prototype, from the user's point of view there is only one form and now users can save and return to form if they want to. This was one of the recommendations in the previous assessment reports. Now, depending on what answers they give to the questions, they are taken to the relevant section of the form and only asked questions that are relevant to them. For example, if a user selects, “Yes, I would like to try early conciliation” then we ask them about the “reason for dispute”. If the user selects “No, I want the certificate to go to the employment tribunal” then users are only asked to enter the full name and address and employer’s legal name and address. Having one form and using progressive disclosure to show relevant questions will save valuable time for both the user and the early conciliation support officer.

Problem to be solved 2:

One of the biggest problems was that sometimes there was a mismatch between the name and address entered on the ACAS certificate and the Employment Tribunal form. The case would get thrown out as a consequence.

How we solved the problem 2:

We have regular coordination meetings with the team at the employment tribunal service to share our learnings from research and see what we can do to improve users' experience of using both services.

As a result of the continuous and ongoing coordination between teams, the new version of the service will allow users to enter the ACAS certificate number and the legal names and addresses of the claimant and respondents will be loaded from the ACAS database. This will reduce the need for users to re-enter data and potentially avoid errors. Users will save valuable time as a result of effective collaboration between two service teams across organisational boundaries. The coordination efforts have also helped improve the guidance text in the forms-based user research learning between the two teams.

Problem to be solved 3:

Another problem was that the users said that they would stop midway while filling the form and be put off by how long the form was. One user said that she was put off for a week.

How we solved the problem 3:

Now instead of asking yes or no to all the reasons for the dispute, we ask the user to select the reason for the dispute and only ask questions relevant to the reason. For example, if the reason for the dispute is “Wages or holiday pay” The user is asked only 3 questions instead of 11 pages of questions, as is on the current form. This will also help allocate the case to the appropriate conciliators who specialist experience in handling certain types of cases.

Constraints

  • Legal – early conciliation is a statutory process with associated legislation
  • Acas does not own the whole case journey. The Employment Tribunal are a separate organisation but our work is inextricably linked.
  • Technical – Drupal and Dynamics CRM are already in place and are not up for review
  • Shared operational processes with Employment Tribunal
  • Possible automated and/or transactional digital service means BEIS and GDS will have significant interest and project will require spend approval at every phase
  • The need to ensure that any process does not create an additional barrier to justice
  • There will be varied and deep stakeholder interest to any changes which may limit or change the options of users to access the conciliation service.

Changes to online and offline service

We obtained the script that early conciliation support officers (ECSOs) use when speaking to a claimant for the first time over the phone. This is a series of questions based on the jurisdiction of a case. Working with these frontline workers we tailored the questions in the form to create a question protocol based on data that frontline workers need. Providing this information to these workers earlier will speed up their processes.

There are BAU processes currently in play to instruct frontline workers of any changes early such as Show and Tells, and Champions (super users) that cascade changes to teams/offices.

How have these mutually benefited each other?

By improving the quality of the data collected in the form, frontline workers will better able to allocate cases to the appropriate team and help users solve their problems in a more timely way. Furthermore, better quality data will open up the possibility of automatic allocation (WS3).

4. Make the service simple to use

How we are building a service that’s simple, intuitive and comprehensible. And test it with users to make sure it works for them.

How have we simplified the existing journey?

Users complained that the existing journey was long winded and asked questions not relevant to them. Using the progressive disclosure, triaging occurs, whereby questions such as whether they are representing themselves, their preferred method of contact, will influence the questions asked. For example, if a user states they just want a certificate then their form will be significantly shorter compared to a user asking for early conciliation. Using progressive disclosure to show relevant questions will save valuable time for both the user and the early conciliation support officer.

Users said that they would stop midway while filling the form and be put off by how long the form was.

Now instead of asking yes or no to all the reasons for the dispute, we ask the user to select the reason for the dispute and only ask questions relevant to the reason. For example, if the reason for the dispute is “Wages or holiday pay” The user is asked only 3 questions instead of 11 pages of questions, as is on the current form. This will also help allocate the case to the appropriate conciliators who specialist experience in handling certain types of cases.

With branching based on the answers to the questions, they don’t have to see questions that are irrelevant to them.

Compared to the current form, feedback we received in testing include:

“That is a hell of a lot faster than the original form, and probably a lot easier than the update. Obviously, it’s just a lot quicker.”

“Because previously in the other form, there is very limited or no space to put an accurate reflection of what the claim is about.”

We have tested our prototypes with claimants and representatives (professional and non-profession) whom have previously completed the existing form. Users were asked how their experience with the prototype compared with the live form. Feedback showed that the process was simpler for users

We’ve also tested with users who had never completed the existing form previously. We got super positive feedack form them too.

7. Use agile ways of working

Create the service using agile, iterative user-centred methods.

We commenced with a 2 week detailed Discovery phase, which took the learnings from the more wide scale Discovery conducted by Deloitte, and focussed on specifically on the ‘Assisted Notifications’ workstream. The output from this mini-Discovery was:

  • A clear set of user needs and user stories
  • A set of hypotheses to test in alpha

The Alpha phase was divided up into 4 x 2 weeks sprints. Prior to year end, our sprints were slightly misaligned with the other workstreams due to differing launch points, but we took advantage of the year-end break to realign the sprint ceremonies for Sprints 3 and 4.

At the start of each sprint, we conducted a planning session with the team and agreed the goal, and scope of the sprint based on estimates formed at planning.

We conducted daily standups for 15mins, where all team members contributed to

  • What they did yesterday
  • What they are doing today
  • Any blockers/impediments

At the conclusion of each sprint, we have a Sprint Review in the form of a show and tell, where invitations were extended right across the ACAS organisation and other workstreams. These sessions were well attended and well-received with audiences of 20+ people.

Following the Sprint Review, we would conduct a Sprint Retro with the immediate team, in order to review our performance and investigate ways of improving processes and delivery.

We used ‘Mentimeter’ to gauge temperatures and discuss outliers, and ‘RetroTool’ to interrogate ‘What went well’, ‘What could improve’, ‘What we should aim to improve form the next sprint’.

Suggested improvements to team ways of working were then monitored at following retros and scored by the team to ensure progress.

9. Create a secure service which protects users’ privacy

How we intend to evaluate data the service will be collecting, storing and providing.

Risk management

At the onset of Discovery we established a RAID log to manage and monitor Risks, Assumptions, Issues and Dependencies. Luke Harrop (Delivery Manager), Kate McCaul (Product Owner) and Matt Williams (Programme Coordinator) would meet fortnightly to:

  • Review open risks and agree continued monitoring status and actions
  • Log new risks and agree likelihood and impact
  • Review open issues and resolution status
  • Test assumptions for continued relevance

Security management

A robust security patching process has been agreed to ensure application and OS vulnerabilities are constantly monitored and addressed to create a configured environment that is secure against known vulnerabilities.

The patching regime covers both core and contributed module security patches that specifically address security vulnerabilities.

It’s important to distinguish functional/feature patches from security patches. We don’t apply ‘functional’ patches as part of our security patching process unless a functional patch also includes a security upgrade, in which case we may need to implement previous feature upgrades at the same time. This will necessitate a larger degree of testing so may require more time from your support allocation than usual.

A security patching policy has been agreed and documented.

Balancing right to privacy with collection of personal information

A key objective of the service is to limit the amount of personal details collected to the minimum necessary to enable the Service teams to provide the level of support required.

The following sensitive data is collected by the service:

  • First and last name of Claimant
  • Claimant’s contact details (email, phone and/or post)
  • Representative’s details (legal, union or family representative)
  • Respondent’s details (the organisation against which a claim is being brought)
  • Other details that are entered into free-text fields

Protection of the users’ data is of paramount importance to the success of the service, as many of the users are in a heightened emotional state when accessing the service and have genuine concerns about the impact that the unauthorised disclosure of their data would have on their situation.

We have worked with the Acas DPO Mark Faulkner to discuss that data that we are intending to collect and its relationship to GDPR, and the means by which we will keep it secure.

Security testing

As part of the beta build activities, we will engage a third party security penetration testing specialist to evaluate the service prior to private beta, and identify any potential security vulnerabilities in line with NCSC guidelines.

Cookie policy implementation

We are advocating a “cross domain” model of consent using OneTrust to allow for robust cookie consent management across the different sites that make up the ACAS content and notifications estate. This means that any website that shares the same parent domain (in this case *.acas.org.uk) can share the same consent. Consent given on one site automatically transfers to consent given on another.

Our proposed approach for Cookie consent management has been documented here.

10. Define what success looks like and publish performance data

What success looks like for our service and metrics which will tell what’s working and what can be improved, combined with user research.

What KPIs have we identified?

  • Digital take up
  • Customer satisfaction
  • Completion rate
  • Cost per transaction
  • Time to allocation
  • Reallocation rates
  • Resolution rates

How were the KPIs derived?

We’ve taken the four standard KPIs and included three others. We’ve derived these KPIs by looking at the expected outcomes and benefits for the programme.

We want to increase early conciliation take up rates to give claimants the best chance to resolve their case before tribunal. Measuring take up rates will allow us to identify if the service is able to persuade more users to opt in to early conciliation.

Time to allocation is important, as an independent evaluation of the service identified that the faster claimants are able to speak to a conciliator, the more likely a settlement becomes.

Additionally, if we help users accurately select their ‘reason for dispute’ we will also reduce the number of cases being recategorised after an initial conversation with the claimant.

If we can collect more complete information about a claimant’s case including dispute type, we will be able to reduce the time to allocation, as we will be able to skip the information gathering stage, which is led by early conciliation support officers (ECSO). This can take time away from focusing on conciliation in the 6 weeks after notification.

What does success look like?

Reduced time to allocation, reduced numbers of cases reallocated, increased take up of early conciliation.

How will we monitor this?

We will baseline these rates on the existing service in private beta. This data will be used by the permanent business-as-usual team to identify improvements to be addressed through continuous improvement.

We have an internal insights team (OPPI - operational performance, planning and insight) as well as a digital performance analyst.

What data are we collecting to help improve the service?

  • Google Analytics will collect usage data
  • We also have the option of using Google Optimize to A-B test content in the pre-notification stage of the journey.
  • Dynamics will track all case data and outcomes
  • Power BI will be used to transform and display the data.

Publishing performance data

We will publish our service data to data.gov.uk

11. Choose the right tools and technology

Tools and technology that we are using to create a high quality service in a cost effective way

Choice of tools and technology

The existing primary service already uses Drupal as it’s web and form capture mechanism. Currently this information is put through a closed source black box integration layer ending up in Microsoft Dynamics which is ACAS’s backoffice case management tool.

After reviewing the existing setup it was determined that Drupal 9 was the best technology choice because of the both existing skill sets within ACAS and because it’s an open source solution and designed on best practice open standards.

From an integration perspective it was determined that the best solution would be to integrate Drupal directly with Microsoft Dynamics as to remove the closed component from the existing structure.`

Other options considered

Wagtail is an open source CMS built in Python it is used in the NHS and the arts. It has a strong emphasis on security and integration. Based on ACAS’s existing sites it was determined a move to Python would create additional overheads from staffing to infrastructure complexity. Also the specific developer community while growing is still small with support focused around a single agency.

Word Press is a CMS written in PHP, it’s one of most widely used CMS in the PHP space. It would fit in with the existing team’s PHP skill set and would have made extending the existing infrastructure straightforward. It was deemed less suitable due to its focus on brochure sites and less of an ability to integrate well. Due to its configuration structure the ability to push configuration up and down stream in the different environments (DEV/TEST/LIVE) is more challenging.

Data security

As part of the Alpha process, we explored the security provisions around the current service, and looked to where risks existed and improvements could be made.

Key areas that we will develop further in Beta are:

  • Investigating a ‘draft’ submission policy, whereby if a draft submission has not been updated for X amount of time, the data is pushed to Dynamics (which is not exposed to the public) and removed from the CMS (which is exposed to the public). When a claimant recalls a draft to complete the submission, the data is pulled back from Dynamics
  • Defining ‘encrypted data at rest’ implementation
  • Defining ‘encrypted data in transit’ implementation
  • Defining process whereby submitted form data is removed from the CMS on successful submission
  • Authentication implementation
  • Data scrubbing for use outside the Production environment (i.e. test, dev)

14. Operate a reliable service

How we intend to minimise service downtime and our plan to deal with it when it does happen

Infrastructure design

CICD

Continuous integration and continuous delivery is our preferred method of development. It allows us to work through logic/functional bugs as well as coding issues. Currently the service uses Jenkins to deliver this functionality. We would propose to extend on this to allow feature environments to be spun up, verified and destroyed throughout the sprint. Based on this model as well as running unit, functional, integration, accessibility and performance tests we will have shippable code with a high level of confidence.

Quality control

During the development phase and in an ongoing BAU test will be written from a unit, functional and integration perspective. As features are built out this body of testing will be run to reduced regressions within the system. Accessibility tools will be run throughout development and in BAU to ensure the service continues to support all users.

Coding reviews will be carried out during the development and BAU phases from a performance, accessibility, open standards and security perspective to ensure ongoing quality of service.
